# Monjo — Brand Strategy

**Author:** Jordan (with strategy assistance)
**Status:** v1, ready to test against the rebuild
**Scope:** This document defines what Monjo is, what it stands for, who buys it, and how every surface of the brand should behave. It is the source of truth the rest of the website, packaging, and copy answer to.

---

## 1. The one-sentence brand

**Monjo makes the most carefully considered objects to write with by hand, in a time when almost nothing is made by hand anymore.**

Everything below either supports this or doesn't belong.

---

## 2. The strategic ground

Three things are true about Monjo right now, and the brand should rest on all three.

**One — the act of writing by hand is rare and getting rarer.** Almost everyone now writes on a keyboard. The people who still pick up a pen are doing it on purpose. They are not buying a notebook because they need one; they are buying it because they want the *act* a notebook makes possible. The brand serves *that intention*, not the abstract need for paper.

**Two — the object itself is unmistakably human.** The cover is painted by Jordan. The signature seal is stamped by Jordan. The spine is sewn by Jordan, with red linen thread. The painting has visible brush wobbles, ink bleeds, water marks. Every Monjo notebook is the trace of a person spending hours on it. In a year where nearly every image and object you encounter is generated by a machine, this matters more than it would have mattered ten years ago. It does not need to be argued — the work argues itself.

**Three — the model is part of the brand.** Each design exists as **one original painting and fifty printed editions of notebooks bearing that painting on the cover**. The original is sold once, to one buyer. The fifty prints make the same image accessible to more people, at a fraction of the price, still in a small, finite run. Once a design's prints are sold, that painting is closed forever. This is the museum model — original + edition — applied to a notebook. It is also the reason a Monjo notebook costs more than a Moleskine and less than a piece of art: because that is what it is.

## 3. Brand name & architecture

Two names need a deliberate relationship.

- **Studio Monjo** is the workshop. The maker. The legal entity. The place in Seoul.
- **Monjo** is the product / the line. The notebook brand.

Use **Monjo** on the product itself, on the back of every notebook, on the spine, in social posts about a specific edition. Use **Studio Monjo** in the workshop, on contracts, in About copy, in conversations about the practice. This is the same distinction as *Hermès* (the house) vs *Birkin* (the object), or *Aesop* (the brand) vs *the company that runs Aesop*. Buyers buy "a Monjo"; they trust "Studio Monjo."

A future second line (cards, prints, workshops, Courrier de Séoul) lives at the same level as Monjo, all under Studio Monjo. *Studio Monjo makes Monjo notebooks, Courrier letters, and ___.*

---

## 4. Brand pillars (the four things to keep saying)

These are the four ideas every page, every product description, every photograph should reinforce. Not all four at once — pick one and let it carry the surface — but never contradict any of them.

**i. Painted by hand, in Seoul.** Every cover image starts as a brush touching paper. Not "inspired by"; literally painted. The hand is the headline.

**ii. Made to be written in, not displayed.** The paper is chosen by someone who writes with fountain pens because Jordan writes with fountain pens. The notebook exists to be filled, not to sit pretty on a shelf. (This is *why* the edition number ended up dropped on the back — to release the object from collector-anxiety.)

**iii. One painting, fifty notebooks. Then it's closed.** The original lives once. The prints live fifty times. After that, the design is gone. This is the structure that makes each notebook feel like an event without making it precious.

**iv. The materials answer to the maker.** Iroful paper from Sakae for fountain-pen fidelity. Heavy Canson for the cover, since 1557. Red Sajou linen thread, visible on the spine. Russian White Nights watercolours. Vermillion ink seal. Each component is the best available answer to a question Jordan actually asked while making the notebooks.

## 5. The customer (who actually buys this)

Not "people who like nice things." Specifically:

- **People who still write by hand.** Journals, letters, drafts, lists. They have favourite pens. They have opinions about paper.
- **Gift-givers buying for someone who still writes by hand.** A child to a parent. A partner to a partner. Someone who knows exactly the kind of person who would treasure this object.
- **Quiet collectors.** People who buy small art editions, who appreciate small craft batches, who own one Postalco bag and one Hobonichi planner and three small ceramic pieces.
- **People reacting to AI-generated everything.** Not loudly, not politically — quietly. They want a thing a person spent hours making. They want their money to land in the bank account of someone who made the thing, not in a server farm. (This audience grows daily. The brand should be ready for them without ever using the words "anti-AI.")

Not the customer: tech-novelty buyers, status-logo seekers, productivity-hack converts. Monjo says nothing to them. That's correct.

## 6. The voice

The voice is **quiet, exact, French-inflected, never precious, never marketing**. It is the voice of someone showing you the thing they made, not the voice of someone selling it.

Words to favour: *cousu, peint, encre, papier, lent, à la main, par, atelier, série, exemplaire, écrire, posé*.

Words to retire: *experience, premium, curated, journey, elevated, mindful, slow living, intentional, artisan* (overused), *crafted* (overused), *bespoke* (overused), *story* (as a noun).

Sentence shape: short, declarative, no flourish, often two beats. *"Painted by hand. In Seoul."* *"One painting. Fifty notebooks. Then it's closed."* *"Pour celles et ceux qui croient encore à la beauté des objets façonnés à la main."* This rhythm — period-and-period — is the brand's signature in language, the way the vermillion seal is its signature in image.

When the voice goes long, it goes long the way a letter goes long: warm, considered, slightly literary, never breathless. See the Courrier de Séoul page for the long form.

## 7. The visual system

**The two registers stay separated.**

- **The container** (the website, the photography of finished objects, the typography, the logo, the chrome) is *austere modern*: bold black sans, generous white space, deep shadow, almost luxury-minimal.
- **The contents** (the paintings on the covers, the dedication line inside, the little character) is *playful and warm*: watercolour washes, brush wobbles, characters with faces.

Holding both is the brand. Don't let the container leak into the contents (no whimsical headers) and don't let the contents leak into the container (no painted UI elements). Their separation is what makes the moment of opening a notebook feel like opening a present.

**Typography.** *Cormorant Garamond* in italic for the literary moments (dedication, hero titles, edition marks). *DM Sans* in 500 weight for labels, navigation, the workmanlike text. Hangul stack pairs with these and follows the same rule. No third typeface.

**Colour.** Paper white `#FAF6ED` (warm cream, not pure white). Ink black `#1A1816`. Seal red `#B8231D` — used *sparingly*, only as accent, never as background. Reserved for: the seal stamp, the spine thread, edition marks, the small "open in Naver" link, one bullet on the dedication eyebrow.

**Photography.** Three registers:
1. **The makings.** Tools on a dark surface: pens, palettes, brushes, sketchbook mid-paint. Tight crops. Shallow depth. Intimate craft.
2. **The objects.** Finished notebooks on a clean light surface, one or two tools nearby. The product, lit gently.
3. **The atmosphere.** A candle, a pen, a page — no product, just mood. Used sparingly. Reserved for full-bleed page moments, never for product cards.

**The signature elements.** Three things are repeated everywhere — they *are* the brand at a glance:
- The **vermillion seal** (印).
- The **red linen spine thread**.
- The **water-drop character** (and his future siblings — see §8).

## 8. The character system (the most important decision in this document)

The water-drop character is not a one-off. It is the first member of a small cast that Jordan paints into every Monjo edition. Some appear on the front cover painting; some are hidden inside the notebook on a single inner page; some recur across editions; some appear only once.

This does several things at once:

- It transforms each notebook from "a botanical painting" into "a small world with a small inhabitant"
- It gives buyers a *reason to come back* — to meet the next one, to find the hidden one
- It gives Monjo a personality that no other fountain-pen-paper notebook has
- It lets Jordan be playful inside an otherwise restrained brand
- It is impossible to fake with AI in a recognizable way, because the cast is yours

The cast can grow slowly. The water-drop is the first. A bird (already appearing in the new paintings). A dragon (already appearing in the new paintings). Future possibilities: a tea-cup, a moon, a paper boat, a small fish. Each one painted in Jordan's hand, each one signed with the seal.

This is the move that takes Monjo from "well-made craft brand" to "brand that people fall in love with."

---

## 9. What Monjo is not

The brand is sharper when these are stated out loud.

- **Not a stationery brand.** A stationery brand sells categories. Monjo sells objects, one design at a time.
- **Not a productivity brand.** No bullet-journal layouts, no habit trackers, no morning-pages templates. The notebook is blank. What you do with it is your business.
- **Not a luxury brand.** Monjo is expensive because making it costs Jordan hours of work, not because the markup is performing exclusivity. The price honours the labour; it does not signal status.
- **Not an "artisan" brand in the marketing sense.** Don't use that word. Show the hand instead.
- **Not an anti-tech statement.** Monjo is for people who write by hand. It is not against anything. It is *for* something.

## 10. How this lands on the website

The current site already does most of this. Three corrections move it further.

1. **Lead with the work, not the words.** The first thing the visitor should see on the Monjo (notebooks) page is a finished notebook, photographed clean and large. Not a headline. Not a manifesto. The painting *is* the headline.

2. **The story of "1 + 50" needs its own moment.** Not a footnote. A dedicated section, with the original painting on one side and a stack of fifty notebooks on the other. This is the model, and the model is the brand.

3. **The character system needs to be named.** Right now the water-drop appears on a cover and nowhere else. A short section — "Le personnage / The inhabitant" — introducing this as a deliberate Monjo convention will reward attentive readers and frame future editions.

The new notebook page structure is laid out in `NOTEBOOK_PAGE_STRUCTURE.md`.

## 11. The decisions that flow from this document

If you ever doubt a choice, run it through these three questions:

1. **Does this honour the hand that made the object?** If not, cut it.
2. **Would this exist if Jordan were not the maker?** If yes — if it's generic brand polish — cut it.
3. **Would the person who still writes by hand recognise themselves in this?** If no, rewrite it.

That's the brand.
