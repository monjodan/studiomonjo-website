Notebooks & cards

Notebooks and cards, painted one at a time, in a Seoul studio.

Made for the page being written on. The paper inside is chosen for the way a fountain-pen line lands on it, and chosen again every edition. The cover is painted in ink and watercolour. The binding is sewn by hand.

The process

A card, painted from start to seal.

Each cover begins as a blank sheet of heavy artist paper. Ink line first, then watercolour, then a small vermillion seal. There's no sketch underneath. The line lands or it doesn't.

Once the cover is dry, the notebook is folded, sewn closed by hand with French waxed linen, numbered inside, signed.

How it writes

Made for the page being written on.

The paper inside is chosen for one thing: the way a fountain-pen line lands on it. Each edition uses a Japanese fountain-pen paper — kind to wet ink, kind to slow strokes. Watercolour holds. Pencil reads. The page doesn't bleed.

What changes between editions: the paper weight, the cover, the size, the colour of the thread. What doesn't: the act of painting one at a time, the hand-binding, the small studio in Seoul where it's made. See the materials ›

By hand

A person makes this. That used to go without saying.

Each cover is the trace of a decision: which way the brush goes, where the line stops, what a hand chose to keep. There's no template, no preview, no second take. The line lands or it doesn't.

What changes from one cover to the next is what changes when a person paints something. What doesn't change is the care. Some things, still, ask to be made slowly, by hand, by someone, for someone.

Materials

What it's made of.

Each piece is built from four materials. They change between editions but stay within a small palette: heavy paper for the cover, fountain-pen paper inside, professional artist watercolours, and waxed linen thread. Each one is chosen for the way it serves the page, the line, or the binding.

Heavy artist paper · France
The cover
Painted in ink and watercolour

A heavy artist sheet from one of the oldest paper traditions in Europe, the kind French watercolourists have been painting on for centuries. Notebook covers use a thick weight that takes ink and wash without buckling. Card covers use a tighter-grain watercolour paper that holds saturated washes better at small format. Both are made to be painted on, not written on.

Fountain-pen paper · Japan
The paper inside
Made for the act of writing

A Japanese fountain-pen paper, smooth but not slick. Ink sits flat on the surface: no feathering, no bleed-through, a clear sheen on the reverse. If you have a favourite pen or a habit of pressing too hard, this paper has you covered. Watercolour holds. Pencil reads. The exact weight and finish are chosen edition by edition.

Artist watercolours · Russia
The paint
Pigment that holds its depth

Professional artist watercolours from a tradition that goes back to the early twentieth century, named after the summer weeks when the northern sun never really sets. The pigment load is unusually high, so colour stays saturated even when watered down for a wash. It keeps its depth rather than fading to a pale stain. The vermillion seal on every cover comes from this same set.

Waxed linen thread · France
The binding
Hand-stitched, edge to edge

Cabled waxed linen, made by a French house that has been turning thread for more than a century and a half. Two already-twisted threads are wound together, then passed through a bath of starch and beeswax. The result is slightly rigid, smooth, and extraordinarily strong — it holds the binding for decades without stretching or fraying.
