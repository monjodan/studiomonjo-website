The practice

Writing by hand changes what you say. You can't go fast. You think in the time it takes the pen to catch up with you, and the thought has to be worth the second it costs to write. The same is true of reading what you wrote later. A handwritten page slows down the reading. The reader gives it the time the writer gave it.

A fountain pen on good paper extends this. The pen tells you about the paper the moment it touches it. The way the nib lifts. The way the ink lays down. The page is quiet, or it isn't. The line will hold, or it will feather. Most paper doesn't notice. A few are listening.

The notebooks I make take a long time. Each cover is painted, each binding sewn by hand. They are slower to make than to use. This is on purpose. A notebook that took attention to make asks for attention to use. The maker and the writer meet in the object, across whatever time has passed between them.

Studio Monjo makes notebooks and cards for that meeting. I paint each cover by hand, in ink and watercolour, in a small studio in Seoul. The paper inside is Iroful, from the Sakae mill that also makes Tomoe River. The binding is hand-stitched with French waxed linen. Twenty of each design, hand-numbered. When the twenty are gone, the design ends.

I make these slowly. They are meant for letters worth keeping, and for the kind of writing you return to.
