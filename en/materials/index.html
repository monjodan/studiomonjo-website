Materials

Three makers,
one notebook.

Every Studio Monjo notebook is assembled from three carefully chosen materials — French cover paper, Japanese interior paper, and Russian watercolours. Each has decades or centuries of craft behind it.

01
France · since 1557

Canson Montgolfier

The cover

Canson is one of the oldest paper mills in Europe. Founded in 1557 by the Montgolfier family of Annonay — yes, the same family that flew the first hot-air balloon in 1782 — the mill became Canson in 1801, when Barthélémy de Canson married into the line.

The cover of every Studio Monjo notebook is cut from Canson artist paper. The sheet is heavy enough to take ink and watercolour wash without buckling, and rough enough that the grain shows through the paint. It ages with the book.

02
Japan · Sakae Technical Paper

Iroful

The paper inside

Iroful is a fountain-pen paper made in Japan by Sakae Technical Paper — the same mill behind the legendary Tomoe River. The name joins iro (colour) and full: a paper that shows off what ink can do.

At 75 gsm, it feels smooth but not slick. Fountain pen ink sits flat — no feathering, no bleed-through, clear sheen on the reverse of the page. If you have a favourite pen, a favourite ink, or a habit of pressing too hard: this paper has you covered. Twenty-four sheets inside every book.

03
Russia · St. Petersburg · since 1934

Nevskaya Palitra

The paint

The watercolours are White Nights, produced by Nevskaya Palitra in Saint Petersburg. The name — Белые Ночи — comes from the summer weeks when the northern sun never really sets over the Neva. They have been made, almost unchanged, since the 1930s.

White Nights is the favourite of generations of Russian painters for good reason: the pigment load is unusually high, which means the colour stays saturated even when it's watered down for a wash. It keeps its depth on the page rather than fading to a pale stain.
