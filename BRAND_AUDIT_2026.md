# Studio Monjo Brand Audit & Positioning Report

Date: June 9, 2026  
Scope: public website, local website repo, local Naver product-description pages, existing strategy docs, and current market/channel research.

## 1. Executive Diagnosis

Studio Monjo is not missing a brand. It has a strong one already. The issue is that the strongest parts are spread across too many names, pages, and strategic directions, so the buyer has to assemble the brand in their head.

The core is clear:

> Studio Monjo makes writing objects by hand in Seoul for people who still write by hand.

That is the center. Everything should point back to it: Robey, Monjo Line, Courrier de Seoul, workshops, future cards, social content, Naver product pages, and the website.

The brand works because it sits at a rare intersection:

- French maker in Seoul
- handwriting in an AI-heavy world
- fountain-pen-friendly paper
- hand-binding and visible process
- small illustrated Seoul stories
- bilingual French/Korean sensibility
- Naver as trusted Korean checkout
- a recurring character system that can become brand IP

The biggest strategic risk is not lack of differentiation. It is dilution. "Stationery", "writing objects", "notebooks and cards", "Robey", "Monjo Line", "Courrier", "workshops", "French stationery house", "AI founder making by hand", "small Korean scenes", and "fountain pen paper" are all true, but they are not currently ordered with enough force.

The job now is to make the hierarchy obvious:

1. Studio Monjo is the house.
2. Writing by hand is the belief.
3. Notebooks are the current commerce engine.
4. Robey in Seoul is the illustrated story series.
5. Monjo Line is the quiet daily/work notebook.
6. Courrier de Seoul is the postal relationship product.
7. Workshops are the in-person proof and community layer.

## 2. Research Signals That Matter

### Korea is the right commerce channel for the notebook line

Korea remains a mobile-first online shopping market. The Ministry of Data and Statistics reported April 2026 online shopping transaction value of KRW 24.1280 trillion, up 10.0 percent year over year, and mobile shopping transaction value of KRW 18.4382 trillion, up 8.6 percent year over year.

Source: Ministry of Data and Statistics, "Online Shopping in April 2026"  
https://mods.go.kr/board.es?act=view&bid=11722&list_no=445346&mainXml=Y&mid=a20101000000

NAVER is also moving deeper into commerce, payment, and AI shopping context. NAVER's Q1 2026 release says service revenue rose 35.6 percent year over year, driven by its commerce ecosystem, and that Npay payment volume reached KRW 24.2 trillion, up 23.4 percent year over year. NAVER also launched an AI Shopping Agent that can suggest products based on saved items, clicks, carts, and trends.

Sources:  
https://www.navercorp.com/en/media/pressReleasesDetail?seq=10034270  
https://www.navercorp.com/en/media/pressReleasesDetail?seq=10034344

Implication: Naver should remain the Korean sales channel. But product details, titles, images, option clarity, and reviews must be written for Naver search and AI-assisted discovery, not only for human readers scrolling a beautiful page.

### Premium analog stationery is not dead

The Korea Times reported that diary decoration is continuing beyond Gen Z into women in their 20s and 30s, and cited 29CM data showing stationery/office-supply transactions up 75 percent year over year from February 1-12, 2025. Premium fountain pens, ballpoint pens, and pencils increased 2.4x; journals and planners grew 64 percent; notebooks grew 43 percent. Point of View's platform sales grew more than 7.6x in the cited period.

Source: Korea Times, "Diary decoration enjoys continued popularity with millennials joining trend"  
https://www.koreatimes.co.kr/lifestyle/trends/20250221/diary-decoration-enjoys-continued-popularity-with-millennials-joining-trend

Implication: Your buyer exists. But the buyer is not "anyone who needs a notebook." It is someone using paper as a chosen ritual, a small luxury, a gift, a writing identity, or an antidote to screen life.

### Handwriting has credible emotional and cognitive support

A 2024 Frontiers in Psychology study reported more elaborate brain connectivity patterns during handwriting than typewriting, with EEG data from 36 university students. The authors connect this to memory formation and encoding new information. This should not be turned into medical claims on product pages, but it supports the brand's intuition that writing by hand feels different because it is different.

Source: Frontiers, "Writing by hand may increase brain connectivity more than typing on a keyboard"  
https://www.frontiersin.org/news/2024/01/26/writing-by-hand-increase-brain-connectivity-typing

Implication: The brand can confidently say that handwriting changes the relationship to thought, memory, and attention. Avoid overclaiming health benefits. Use this as a foundation for tone, not as sales proof.

### Analog journaling is a material and identity practice

A 2025 HCI paper on analog journaling describes paper journaling as involving planning, habit-tracking, reflecting, personal tool preferences, layout choices, memory, identity, and community.

Source: "Crafting a Personal Journaling Practice: Negotiating Ecosystems of Materials, Personal Context, and Community in Analog Journaling"  
https://arxiv.org/abs/2504.19767

Implication: Your product pages should not only say "good paper." They should show what the notebook helps someone become: a person who notices, remembers, thinks, writes, reads, plans, or sends.

### Courrier de Seoul has a real emotional market

Fondation de France's 2025 solitude study reports that in 2024, 12 percent of French people aged 15+ were in relational isolation, and 24 percent felt regularly lonely. La Poste's 2025 annual results report continued structural decline in mail, with mail volumes down 8.1 percent and mail representing only 15 percent of revenue.

Sources:  
https://www.fondationdefrance.org/fr/cat-personnes-vulnerables/etude-solitudes-2025  
https://www.lapostegroupe.com/fr/actualite/resultats-2025-du-groupe-la-poste

Implication: Courrier should not be positioned as content. It is a recurring relationship object. The fact that letters are rarer makes the gesture stronger.

### Seoul craft and stationery experiences are attractive

Visit Seoul describes Point of View Seoul in Seongsu as a concept stationery store with paper, writing instruments, creative tools, and three themed floors. KTO's 2026 tourism reporting points toward more data-driven, taste-based tourism, with foreign visitors building itineraries around specific lifestyle patterns such as Seongsu cafes and Korean desserts.

Sources:  
https://english.visitseoul.net/shopping/POINT-OF-VIEW-SEOUL/ENP01flpb  
https://en.sedaily.com/culture/2026/05/19/korea-tourism-organization-launches-quarterly-travel

Implication: Workshops can be more than an add-on. They can become the proof that Studio Monjo is a living Seoul studio, not just a Naver shop.

## 3. Current Brand Strengths

### The strongest promise

The strongest promise is not "premium stationery."

It is:

> A human-made writing object, from Seoul, for people who still write by hand.

This promise is emotionally current because it answers three modern needs:

- attention in a distracted world
- touch in a digital world
- human trace in an AI-heavy world

This is a good foundation because it does not require nostalgia. It is not saying "technology is bad." It says "the hand still has a role."

### The visual language is distinctive

The Robey cover image is strong because it is not generic botanical stationery. It has a small character, a scene, a specific quietness. The Monjo Line image is strong because it gives the opposite register: blank, disciplined, practical, still human through the binding and mark. Courrier has its own sensory material signal through hanji, typed pages, blue floral paper, and monthly dating.

The brand can hold two visual modes:

- restrained house system
- warm illustrated inner world

That contrast is a real asset.

### The maker story is defensible

The AI-to-handmade arc is strong, but it must be handled carefully. It is a moat when it appears as a quiet biographical fact. It becomes a liability if it sounds like a manifesto, status claim, or anti-AI performance.

Use it like this:

> Jordan worked for years in artificial intelligence. Studio Monjo is the other side of that work: objects made slowly, by hand, for someone else to write in by hand.

Do not use it like this:

> In a world dominated by soulless AI, Studio Monjo fights back through authentic craft.

The first is trustworthy. The second feels generic and reactive.

## 4. Main Sources of Confusion

### 1. Public website and local repo do not tell the same product story

The live public `/en/notebooks/` page currently positions the notebook page around Robey and Falling Petals only. The local repo page positions two families: Robey in Seoul and Monjo Line, with three current Robey editions and Monjo Line.

This is not a small detail. If Naver sells Robey 001/002/003 and Monjo Line, while the public website explains only one Robey page, the brand feels behind its own shop.

Recommendation: either deploy the current local notebook architecture quickly, or simplify the Naver shop until the website catches up. The website should be the explanation layer for exactly what Naver sells.

### 2. "Writing Objects" is poetic but not self-explanatory

On the homepage, "Writing Objects" is elegant, but it does not immediately say what the buyer can buy. The supporting line mentions stationery and Robey, but the first-screen label still asks for interpretation.

Recommendation:

- Use "Notebooks" or "Notebooks & cards" on the homepage door.
- Use "Writing objects" as the higher-level category name on the destination page.

Homepage labels should be clear before they are beautiful. Internal pages can become more poetic after the visitor has chosen a door.

### 3. "Monjo" is being used at too many levels

Existing docs once define Monjo as the notebook product line. The current repo uses "Monjo Line" as a minimal notebook family. The house is Studio Monjo. Buyers could easily ask:

- Is Monjo the brand?
- Is Studio Monjo the brand?
- Is Monjo Line the product?
- Is Robey the brand?

Recommendation: retire "Monjo" as the generic product-line name. Use this architecture:

| Level | Name | Role |
| --- | --- | --- |
| House | Studio Monjo | The maker and trust source |
| Belief/category | Writing by hand | The reason everything exists |
| Commerce family | Writing Objects | The umbrella for notebooks/cards |
| Illustrated notebook series | Robey in Seoul | Story notebooks |
| Minimal notebook series | Monjo Line | Daily/work notebooks |
| Postal product | Courrier de Seoul | Monthly letter |
| Experience product | Studio Monjo Workshops | In-person making |

### 4. "Edition" currently implies scarcity without an exact rule

Robey pages use "Edition 001/002/003", but the newer local site copy says the date is not scarcity and that Studio Monjo can keep, pause, or retire designs. Earlier strategy docs suggested one original painting plus 50 notebooks, then closed.

Both models can work, but they are different businesses.

Choose one:

Model A: limited art editions  
One painting, 50 notebooks, closed forever. Stronger collector logic, higher price ceiling, more operational pressure.

Model B: dated story series  
Each design has a release number/date, made to order while active, can be paused or retired. Better for Naver, simpler inventory, less collector anxiety.

Recommendation for now: use Model B. It matches your Naver pages and gives you flexibility. Replace scarcity language with "dated illustrated series" and "small-batch, made to order." Only use "limited" if you commit to a number.

## 5. Recommended Brand Positioning

### Core house statement

> Studio Monjo is a French writing house in Seoul, making notebooks, letters, and small workshops for people who still write by hand.

Why this works:

- "French" differentiates.
- "Seoul" grounds.
- "Writing house" is broader than stationery but less vague than studio.
- "Notebooks, letters, workshops" tells people what exists.
- "People who still write by hand" is the emotional target.

### Shortest line

> For people who still write by hand.

This is already strong. Keep it.

### Product-system line

> Hand-bound notebooks, monthly letters, and small workshops from a French studio in Seoul.

Use this when people need concrete comprehension fast.

### Korean positioning direction

Korean copy should be less translated and more buyer-clear:

> 스튜디오 몬조는 서울에서 손으로 만드는 필기 작업실입니다. 만년필로 쓰기 좋은 노트, 매달 도착하는 편지, 작은 워크숍을 만듭니다.

Shorter:

> 손으로 쓰는 사람을 위한 서울의 필기 작업실.

For Naver:

> 서울에서 주문 후 손으로 제본하는 만년필용 노트.

Naver needs less literary atmosphere at the top and more purchase clarity.

## 6. Product Niches

### A. Robey in Seoul

Niche:

> Illustrated, hand-bound, fountain-pen-friendly Seoul story notebooks for journaling, gifting, travel memories, and people who notice small scenes.

This should not compete with Moleskine, Muji, Leuchtturm, Midori, or generic Korean diary brands on utility alone. Robey wins by combining utility with a character-world and Seoul narrative.

Primary buyers:

- Korean women and men in their 20s-30s who journal, decorate diaries, use fountain pens, or buy thoughtful stationery
- gift buyers looking for "small but meaningful"
- people who like illustrated books, Korea/Seoul stories, and gentle characters
- tourists, expats, and Francophone/Korea-culture buyers who want a Seoul object that is not souvenir-like

Job to be done:

- "I want a notebook that makes writing feel softer and more meaningful."
- "I want a gift that looks personal without needing personalization."
- "I want to remember Seoul or a season in a small physical object."

What makes it defensible:

- Robey as recurring character/IP
- Korean scenes seen through a French resident's eye
- bilingual story card
- visible hand-binding and thread color
- fountain-pen-friendly paper
- Naver convenience

What to tighten:

- Put "A5/A6, 48 pages, blank/lined/grid, made to order, fountain-pen-friendly" near the top.
- Move "who makes it" lower or compress it.
- Add an early "choose this if" block.
- Make each edition's use case more distinct.

Recommended Naver listing-title pattern:

> [러비 인 서울 001] 손제본 만년필 노트 A5/A6 - 떨어지는 꽃잎

Top product-page line:

> 서울의 작은 계절 장면을 담은 일러스트 손제본 노트입니다. A5/A6, 무지/줄/모눈 속지, 48쪽, 만년필 필기 테스트를 거친 종이로 주문 후 제작합니다.

Do not overuse "다꾸" in body copy. It may help search, but it shifts the brand toward sticker/decoration culture. Use it as a tag if needed, not as voice.

### B. Robey Edition 001 - Falling Petals

Niche:

> The quiet journaling/gift edition.

Best target:

- first-time Studio Monjo buyer
- journal writers
- people buying a gentle gift
- people who like seasonal imagery

Positioning:

> A notebook for small pauses: petals, windows, quiet thoughts.

Korean buyer language:

> 잠깐 멈춰 쓰고 싶은 사람을 위한 첫 번째 러비 노트.

Conversion angle:

- "first scene"
- "red thread"
- "quiet daily journal"
- "giftable story card"

Improve:

- Add "첫 번째 장면" high on the page.
- Bring gift use earlier.
- Show a quick "추천: 일기, 필사, 선물, 여행 기록" row near the first image.

### C. Robey Edition 002 - Orange Balloon

Niche:

> The light, playful, wishful edition.

Best target:

- younger gift buyer
- daily notes
- casual journaling
- someone who wants Robey to feel more optimistic

Positioning:

> A notebook for small wishes and lighter days.

Korean buyer language:

> 무거운 생각보다 가볍게 적어두고 싶은 날을 위한 러비 노트.

Conversion angle:

- orange thread matched to balloon
- "small wishes"
- warmer and more playful than Edition 001

Improve:

- Make the difference from Edition 001 obvious in the first 200 words.
- Use "일상 기록", "작은 소원", "가벼운 선물" in Naver detail and search tags.

### D. Robey Edition 003 - Small Library

Niche:

> The reading notebook.

This is the easiest Robey edition to target clearly.

Best target:

- readers
- book club people
- students and language learners
- people who annotate, copy quotes, keep reading lists
- gift buyers for readers

Positioning:

> A small library for thoughts gathered from books.

Korean buyer language:

> 읽은 문장과 생각을 모아두는 독서 노트.

Conversion angle:

- "독서노트"
- "책 메모"
- "읽은 문장 필사"
- navy thread
- story card

Improve:

- Put "독서노트" in the Naver title or subtitle.
- Add use-case photos: book + pen + notebook, reading list page, copied quote page.
- If any edition can create repeat sales by use-case, this is it.

Recommended listing:

> [러비 인 서울 003] 손제본 만년필 독서 노트 A5/A6 - 작은 도서관

### E. Monjo Line

Niche:

> Minimal hand-bound fountain-pen notebooks for work, meetings, project notes, planning, and quiet daily carry.

Monjo Line should be the profit engine. It is less emotionally specific than Robey, but easier to buy repeatedly and easier to give in professional contexts.

Primary buyers:

- founders, designers, consultants, writers, academics, and knowledge workers
- people who want a good notebook without illustration
- fountain pen users who need practical paper
- corporate/business gift buyers who want restrained taste

Job to be done:

- "I want a notebook I can bring to meetings that does not feel childish."
- "I want a daily paper tool that respects my fountain pen."
- "I want a quiet gift for someone who writes, works, or thinks on paper."

Positioning:

> The Studio Monjo notebook without the story on the cover.

Korean:

> 일러스트 없이, 쓰는 일에 집중한 손제본 업무 노트.

Recommended Naver listing:

> [몬조 라인] 손제본 만년필 업무 노트 A5/A6 - 무지/줄/모눈

What makes it defensible:

- handmade without looking decorative
- fountain-pen paper
- black thread
- tiny mark
- French writing-culture signal
- business gift potential

What to improve:

- Add "업무용" and "회의 노트" higher.
- Add a corporate gift inquiry path if you can fulfill it.
- Make the product less dependent on the founder story and more dependent on use cases.
- Consider selling multipacks or "work pair" bundles after review data supports demand.

Monjo Line should not compete with Muji on price. It should compete on "quiet object that respects the hand."

### F. Courrier de Seoul

Niche:

> A monthly physical letter from Seoul for French-speaking people to receive or gift to someone they love, especially someone who cannot travel.

This is not a newsletter. It is not stationery. It is not a postcard club. It is a relationship product.

Primary buyers:

- French adults with parents/grandparents who no longer travel
- people with a loved one in another city
- Francophones interested in Korea but tired of feeds and content
- people buying an intimate gift that repeats monthly

Job to be done:

- "I want my grandmother/parent/friend to receive something from me, even when I am far away."
- "I want a reason to wait for the mailbox again."
- "I want Seoul in a form that is gentle and readable."

Positioning:

> Une visite par la poste, depuis Seoul.

The current Courrier page is the strongest page in the system because it knows exactly who it is for. Keep the emotional clarity.

What to improve:

- Add a slightly clearer "French only" signal on the multilingual homepage.
- Make the subscription logistics even easier to scan: ships second week, France metropolitan, same envelope for self/gift.
- Consider a gift-message field or post-payment email template because the primary buyer is often gifting.
- Be careful with operational load. This product has recurring obligation and postage fragility.

Pricing role:

- 3 months at 40 EUR: trial/gift
- 12 months at 120 EUR: relationship commitment

This can become brand-defining even if it is not the biggest revenue line.

### G. Workshops

Niche:

> A premium small-group notebook-making workshop in Seoul: fold, stitch, paint, sign, and take home an A6 notebook.

Primary buyers:

- Seoul-based expats and creative locals
- tourists looking for a small studio experience
- couples/friends seeking a quiet activity
- stationery/fountain-pen people
- companies or teams wanting a tasteful small creative workshop, if you choose to serve them

Job to be done:

- "I want to make something real with my hands in Seoul."
- "I want a memory of Korea that I made, not bought."
- "I want a calm workshop that feels premium, not a tourist craft factory."

Positioning:

> Three hours. One notebook. Your hand in the object.

Korean:

> 세 시간, 노트 한 권. 직접 접고 꿰매고 그립니다.

What makes it defensible:

- direct contact with the maker
- Studio Monjo materials
- premium small size, max six seats
- English/French/Korean potential
- good content engine for Instagram/YouTube

What to improve:

- Add actual dates or an email waitlist. "Booking link will appear" is too passive.
- State languages clearly.
- Decide whether the workshop is public, private, or both.
- Add one "for teams/private groups" line only if operationally desirable.

Pricing:

KRW 150,000 per seat is plausible for a premium small studio workshop if the page shows intimacy, materials, and a finished object. The price needs visible outcomes and date certainty.

### H. Future Cards / Letter Sets

The homepage mentions notebooks and cards, but the current shop focus is notebooks. This creates a promise gap if cards are not available.

Recommendation:

- If cards will launch within 30 days, keep "notebooks & cards."
- If not, change homepage to "Notebooks" or "Writing Objects" and mention cards only as "to follow" on the notebook page.

Best niche for cards:

> Sendable Seoul scenes from Robey's world.

Do not make them generic greeting cards. Make them the correspondence branch of Robey.

## 8. Competitive Landscape

Studio Monjo should not try to beat competitors on every dimension. It should choose the territory where the combination is hard to copy.

| Competitive set | They win on | Studio Monjo can win on |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Muji, Artbox, Kyobo basics | price, availability, simple utility | not the target; do not compete |
| Midori, Hobonichi, Leuchtturm, Rhodia, Clairefontaine | paper reputation, global trust, systems | story, Seoul, handmade, character, local gift |
| Point of View / curated premium stationery | retail experience, curation, range | maker-led product, personal world, direct story |
| Korean design stationery brands | cute/design variety, trend speed | quieter premium identity, fountain-pen proof, French-Seoul angle |
| Etsy/local handmade notebooks | handmade uniqueness | stronger brand system, Naver trust, repeatable product families |
| Tourist craft workshops | accessibility, variety | premium small studio, real product continuity |
| Digital notes/apps | speed, search, storage | attention, touch, memory, meaning |

White space:

> A maker-led French writing house in Seoul where premium paper, hand-binding, small Korean scenes, and a recurring character become useful writing objects.

That is narrow enough to be memorable and broad enough to grow.

## 9. Moat

### Strong moats

1. Founder-market fit  
The founder's AI background and French/Seoul identity make the hand-made writing-object idea believable. A random stationery brand cannot copy that without sounding false.

2. Robey as IP  
Robey is the most scalable emotional asset. Materials can be copied. A character-world, built slowly across editions, is harder to copy.

3. Bilingual bridge  
French/Korean is not decoration. It creates a cultural lane: Seoul seen by a French maker, explained through paper and correspondence.

4. Material proof  
Paper tests, binding GIFs, thread details, stamps, and story cards create trust. Show them constantly.

5. Naver trust plus website world  
Naver handles checkout in the trusted local mode. The website provides the world and depth Naver cannot carry alone.

6. Workshops  
Workshops turn the brand from product to place. They create content, referrals, customer intimacy, and authority.

### Weak or fake moats

1. "Fountain-pen-friendly" alone  
Many brands can claim this. It is a requirement, not a moat.

2. "Handmade" alone  
Many products are handmade. The moat is handmade by this person, in this world, with this character, for this buyer.

3. Scarcity without rule  
"Edition" only becomes a moat if the rule is clear and trusted.

4. Naver store alone  
Naver is distribution, not brand defensibility.

5. French luxury language  
Too much "French" can become surface-level. Use it through restraint, paper culture, correspondence, and voice.

## 13. Pricing and Profit Role

This is strategic guidance, not financial advice. Exact pricing needs cost, time, material, defect, shipping, and Naver fee data.

### Robey

Role:

- brand love
- gift conversion
- character/IP growth
- premium story product

- should be above generic notebooks
- should not look like mass stationery
- can increase if packaging, story card, edition logic, and reviews support it

### Monjo Line

- repeat purchase
- business/corporate gift potential
- simpler production than Robey

Pricing logic:

Opportunity:

- bundles
- multipacks
- meeting/project sets
- small corporate orders

### Courrier

Role:

- recurring relationship product
- French sub-brand
- deep emotional identity

Risk:

- monthly obligation, postage, address management, international logistics

Opportunity:

- gift periods
- seasonal push
- annual plan retention
- eventually archive/print compilation if demand exists

### Workshops

Role:

- high-touch acquisition
- content engine
- premium experience
- proof of studio

## 16. Open Decisions

1. Is "edition" a limited quantity or a story number?

This must be decided. It affects copy, price, and customer expectation.

2. Is "Monjo Line" the final name?

It works, but it creates some ambiguity with Studio Monjo. If kept, define it clearly as the minimal daily notebook line.

3. Are cards currently real?

If not, remove them from first-screen homepage language until launch.

4. Do you want international product buyers?

Naver is good for Korean buyers but difficult for many international customers. If international buyers matter, add an email/order inquiry path or a separate lightweight checkout later.

5. Is the workshop a recurring product or occasional event?

If recurring, add scheduling/waitlist infrastructure. If occasional, position it as "opened by date" and collect interest.

6. Is Robey a character brand?

The answer should be yes. Robey is the thing that can make people return.

## 17. Final Strategic Recommendation

The cleanest profitable path is:

1. Make Monjo Line the practical/repeatable notebook.
2. Make Robey the emotional/gift/character notebook.
3. Make Courrier the French relationship product.
4. Make Workshops the proof that the studio is real.
5. Use the website to explain the world.
6. Use Naver to convert Korean buyers.
7. Use Instagram/YouTube/Naver Blog to show the hand, the paper, and the character.

The brand should not become broader. It should become clearer.

The soul is already there:

> a French person in Seoul, after years building with machines, making paper objects by hand for people who still write by hand.

That is enough. The work now is to put that sentence in the right order on every surface.
