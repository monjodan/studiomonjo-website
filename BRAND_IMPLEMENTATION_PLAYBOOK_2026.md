# Studio Monjo Brand Implementation Playbook

Date: June 9, 2026  
Purpose: implementation layer for the brand audit. This document answers how much to lean into the founder, how to use image and color, how the brand should sound, and how to run content and outreach without losing the soul of Studio Monjo.

## 1. The Implementation Principle

Studio Monjo should be founder-led, but not founder-centered.

The founder should be present as evidence:

- evidence that the notebooks are made by hand
- evidence that the letters are written, signed, assembled, and sent by a person
- evidence that the workshops are intimate and trustworthy
- evidence that the brand comes from a real life between France, Seoul, writing, and making

The founder should not become the product.

The product remains:

- the notebook someone writes in
- the letter someone receives
- the workshop someone remembers
- the small Seoul scene someone carries
- the feeling that writing by hand still matters

The correct balance is:

> The founder opens the door. The objects carry the brand.

## 2. How Much To Lean Into Yourself

### Recommended Founder Visibility Level

Use yourself at a level of 3 out of 5.

Do not hide. Do not become an influencer-persona brand.

In practical terms:

- 15 to 25 percent of public content can include your face, voice, hands, or personal story.
- 50 to 60 percent should show objects, process, writing, paper, Seoul details, and use cases.
- 15 to 20 percent should show Robey, monthly stories, customer writing rituals, workshops, and community.
- The remaining 5 to 10 percent can be direct business updates: launches, ordering windows, workshop dates, shipping notes, restocks.

The face should appear less often than the hand.

The hand is more powerful for Studio Monjo because the brand is about writing, folding, binding, painting, signing, and sending. Your face builds trust, but your hands explain the brand faster.

### Where Your Face Should Appear

Use a clear founder image in these places:

1. Website about/studio section
2. Workshop page
3. One pinned Instagram post
4. One Naver Smart Store profile or brand introduction image
5. Occasional process videos where you are visibly making
6. Press kit or collaboration deck
7. A small maker card insert if it feels personal and not too self-promotional

Avoid using your face as the main image for:

- Naver product thumbnails
- notebook hero images
- Courrier hero images
- every Instagram Reel cover
- every launch announcement

The product must remain visually primary at the moment of buying.

### Where Your Hands Should Appear

Use hands often:

- folding paper
- sewing binding thread
- cutting covers
- writing a title
- testing ink
- opening a notebook flat
- placing the story card
- signing a Courrier letter
- sealing an envelope
- preparing workshop materials
- handing a finished notebook to a participant

Hands are the correct founder image for a craft brand because they make the promise visible.

### The Founder Story To Tell

The founder story should be a quiet contrast, not a manifesto.

Best version:

> I spent years working with artificial intelligence. Studio Monjo is the other side of that work: slow objects, made by hand in Seoul, for people who still write by hand.

This is strong because it is specific, contemporary, and restrained.

Weak version:

> In a soulless digital world, Studio Monjo fights back with authentic handmade stationery.

This is weaker because it sounds generic and overdramatic.

### Personal Details To Use

Use:

- French maker in Seoul
- trained eye for systems and detail
- AI background as contrast
- love of handwriting, paper, letters, scenes, and rituals
- your role as the person who designs, binds, writes, packs, teaches
- why Korea and France both matter to the brand

Do not overuse:

- founder biography as status
- productivity ideology
- anti-technology language
- "I quit everything to follow my passion" framing
- suffering, burnout, or hustle as brand fuel
- lifestyle content unrelated to writing, making, Seoul, letters, paper, or workshops

The public should feel there is a real person behind the house. They should not feel asked to follow your private life.

## 3. Founder Image Rules

### Image Types To Create

Create a founder image set with five categories.

#### 1. Studio Portrait

Purpose: trust and press.

Shot:

- you in the studio
- neutral clothes
- warm daylight
- visible table, tools, notebooks, paper, or shelves
- looking at camera for one version
- looking down at work for another version

Use:

- About page
- press kit
- workshop page
- pinned introduction

Do not over-style it. It should feel like a maker in a working room, not a founder headshot for a startup.

#### 2. Hands At Work

Purpose: proof of craft.

Shot:

- close crop
- paper texture visible
- thread, needle, pen, brush, stamp, envelope
- stable framing
- natural imperfections acceptable

Use:

- Naver detail page
- Instagram Reels
- Naver Blog
- product launch posts
- workshop page

This should become the most repeated human image category.

#### 3. Over-Shoulder Writing

Purpose: explain the writing belief.

Shot:

- your shoulder or arm visible
- pen touching paper
- notebook in use
- enough negative space for captions or crop

Use:

- handwriting content
- Monjo Line
- Courrier
- "why blank/lined/grid" posts

#### 4. Packing And Sending

Purpose: trust and care.

Shot:

- wrapping notebooks
- placing story card
- writing recipient names
- assembling Courrier
- stacking outgoing envelopes

Use:

- stories
- customer reassurance
- launch week
- review prompts

#### 5. Workshop Host

Purpose: lower the barrier to attending.

Shot:

- you explaining materials
- participant hands, with permission
- table with tools
- finished notebooks
- small group, calm room

Use:

- workshop page
- workshop outreach
- Instagram highlights
- Naver Blog workshop recap

### Image Rules

Use:

- daylight or warm studio light
- real paper texture
- real tools
- stable compositions
- close details
- occasional face, frequent hands
- backgrounds that show Seoul or the studio only when they add specificity

Avoid:

- heavy blur as default
- generic coffee-shop lifestyle shots
- dark images where the product cannot be inspected
- influencer-style mirror shots
- exaggerated cinematic mood that hides the object
- overly clean luxury product photography with no evidence of human hand

The visual standard is:

> quiet, tactile, specific, useful.

## 5. Typography And Layout Tone

The current typography direction is good:

- serif for atmosphere
- sans for structure
- Korean sans/serif used with care
- restrained uppercase labels
- generous spacing

Implementation refinement:

- Use serif for poetic titles, Courrier, Robey story, and emotional lines.
- Use sans for buying information, specs, navigation, Naver clarity, workshop details, and practical explanation.
- For Korean product pages, prioritize legibility and purchase clarity over European atmosphere.
- Keep letter spacing modest. Korean especially should not feel spaced out or decorative.

The website can be more poetic than Naver. Naver needs to sell clearly.

## 6. Tone Of Voice

### Brand Voice

Studio Monjo should sound:

- precise
- warm
- observant
- restrained
- sensory
- practical when needed
- lightly poetic, never vague

The voice should not sound:

- cute
- luxury-generic
- startup-like
- anti-digital
- overly literary
- therapeutic
- academic
- like a mass-market stationery brand

### The Sentence Pattern

Use short, clear sentences for product truth.

Example:

> Each notebook is folded, stitched, and checked by hand in Seoul.

Use slightly more poetic sentences for emotional meaning.

Example:

> Robey appears in small Seoul scenes for people who still like to notice things slowly.

Use direct practical language for Naver.

Example:

> A5 is best for journaling, study notes, and longer writing. A6 is easier to carry for daily notes and travel.

### First Person Or We?

Use "Studio Monjo" or "we" for the house.

Use "I" only when the founder is speaking directly:

- founder note
- process caption
- workshop invitation
- Courrier monthly note
- about page
- email or letter

Rule:

> The brand says "we". The maker sometimes says "I".

This keeps the house bigger than one person while preserving intimacy.

### Words To Own

Use often:

- write by hand
- made by hand in Seoul
- folded
- stitched
- signed
- paper
- fountain pen
- letter
- notebook
- small scene
- monthly
- workshop
- quiet
- useful
- remember
- carry
- send

### Words To Use Carefully

Use only when concrete:

- premium
- sustainable
- archival
- limited
- one of a kind
- artisan
- luxury
- authentic
- slow living
- mindful

These words are overused. Studio Monjo is stronger when it proves them through details.

### Words To Avoid

Avoid:

- hustle
- aesthetic as a noun
- vibe
- romanticize everything
- handcrafted with love
- perfect gift for everyone
- escape the digital world
- productivity hack
- self-care essential
- must-have

Studio Monjo should never sound desperate for attention.

## 7. Content Strategy

### The Content Job

Content has four jobs:

1. Make people understand what you sell.
2. Make people believe it is truly made by hand.
3. Make the products feel useful in their own life.
4. Make the world of Studio Monjo worth returning to.

Do not create content only to post.

Each post should answer at least one of these:

- What is this object?
- Who is it for?
- How is it made?
- How do I use it?
- Why does it feel different?
- What story does it carry?
- Why should I trust this maker?
- Why should I buy now?

### Five Content Pillars

#### Pillar 1: The Hand

Purpose: proof.

Formats:

- folding paper
- sewing thread
- cutting covers
- checking pages
- stamping
- signing story cards
- wrapping orders
- workshop table setup

Good captions:

> Folding the pages before stitching. This is the moment the notebook starts to behave like an object.

> Red thread for Falling Petals. Small detail, but it changes the whole feeling of the cover.

#### Pillar 2: The Page

Purpose: usefulness.

Formats:

- fountain pen tests
- blank/lined/grid comparison
- A5 versus A6
- writing desk
- reading notes
- meeting notes
- journaling setup
- ink dry time and paper handling

Good captions:

> Blank if you want space. Lined if you want rhythm. Grid if your thoughts arrive in fragments.

> Monjo Line was made for the notebook that stays open beside the work.

#### Pillar 3: The Scene

Purpose: Robey world and Seoul specificity.

Formats:

- small Seoul observations
- Robey cover story
- window, library, balloon, flower details
- short bilingual story fragments
- "where the edition started"

Good captions:

> Robey does not explain Seoul. He notices it.

> Edition 003 began with a small library and the feeling of carrying a book home.

#### Pillar 4: The Letter

Purpose: Courrier conversion and emotional depth.

Formats:

- monthly topic teaser
- envelope assembly
- postcard detail
- hanji texture
- why send to someone
- recipient moments, if shared with permission
- behind the monthly route/story

Good captions:

> Courrier is not a newsletter. It is a letter someone can hold.

> This month leaves Seoul in the second week.

#### Pillar 5: The Table

Purpose: workshops and community.

Formats:

- materials laid out
- participant hands
- finished notebooks
- small group moments
- before/after
- dates and seats
- workshop recap

Good captions:

> Six seats, one table, one A6 notebook made from the first fold to the final cover.

> A workshop is the easiest way to understand why the binding matters.

- What is Studio Monjo?
- What can I buy?
- Why is it different?
- Where do I buy it?

### Naver Smart Store

Role:

- Korean checkout
- product discovery
- reviews
- conversion
- Naver search and AI shopping context

Naver should be more practical than the website.

Implementation:

- Put product type and search keywords in title.
- Put recommendation/use-case block near the top.
- Put size/page-style decision guide before the long maker story.

- Use product video or short clips where possible.
- Use tags and video titles carefully.
- Fill Page title and Meta description for share cards.
- Ask for reviews with photos, especially fountain pen use and gift context.

Naver's own help notes that product search quality involves relevance, popularity, and trust, and that tags/Page title/Meta description matter for product sharing and search settings. Naver also notes product videos can appear in product detail and search contexts when relevant tags and video titles are entered.

Do not look for influencers first. Look for people whose audience already believes in writing, paper, Seoul, books, gifts, or quiet craft.

The best outreach target is not the biggest account.

The best outreach target is someone who can naturally say:

> I understand why this exists.

### 90-Day Outreach Targets

Build a list of 60 targets:

- 15 Korean fountain pen/stationery bloggers or creators
- 10 journaling/book accounts
- 10 Seoul lifestyle/travel accounts
- 10 French/Korea cultural accounts
- 5 boutique shops or bookstores
- 5 workshop/culture platforms
- 5 professional/community partners

Send 10 thoughtful messages per week for 6 weeks.

Expected outcomes:

- 10 to 20 replies
- 5 to 8 samples or visits
- 3 to 5 useful pieces of content
- 1 to 2 durable partnerships

This is enough for a small brand. You do not need viral scale first.

## 16. Founder Visibility Decision Rules

Use your face when:

- trust is the barrier
- someone is deciding whether to attend a workshop
- you are telling the origin story
- press or partners need to understand who is behind the house
- you are making a direct invitation

Use your hands when:

- craft is the proof
- product value is the question
- the post is about making
- the buyer needs to feel texture and process

Use the object alone when:

- purchase clarity is the goal
- Naver thumbnail or product hero needs inspection
- comparing editions
- showing options/specs

Use Robey when:

- building emotional memory
- telling Seoul stories
- softening the brand
- making collectible continuity

Use the customer/participant when:

- proving use
- showing workshop outcomes
- building community
- showing scale beyond yourself

## 17. The Brand Boundary

Studio Monjo should not become:

- a personal lifestyle account
- a generic stationery shop
- a cute character goods brand
- a productivity brand
- a nostalgic anti-tech brand
- a discount-driven Naver shop
- a content machine about small business ownership

Studio Monjo should become:

- a small writing house
- a trusted handmade notebook maker
- a quiet Seoul story world
- a monthly letter ritual
- a workshop table people want to sit at
- a brand people follow because the objects and scenes feel specific

The strongest implementation line remains:

> Founder-led enough to be trusted. Object-led enough to become a brand.
