# Studio Monjo Distinctiveness Layer

Date: June 9, 2026  
Purpose: make the brand more memorable, ownable, and harder to confuse with other premium stationery or handmade notebook brands.

## 1. The Honest Problem

The current strategy is coherent, but coherence is not enough.

"For people who still write by hand" is true, but it is not yet ownable. Kakimori can say something close. TRAVELER'S COMPANY can say something close. Hobonichi can say something close. Many independent notebook makers can say something close.

Studio Monjo needs a sharper memory hook.

The hook is not simply:

> handwriting

It is:

> Seoul, written by hand.

Or, more emotionally:

> small scenes from Seoul, made to be written, sent, and kept.

This is more specific because it combines:

- place: Seoul
- hand: made and written by hand
- form: notebooks, letters, workshops
- character: Robey as the small observer
- founder: French maker in Seoul, coming from AI into handwork
- ritual: scenes become objects, objects become writing

That combination is harder to copy than "premium handmade stationery."

## 2. Competitive Reality

### TRAVELER'S COMPANY owns journey

TRAVELER'S COMPANY says its products share the theme of traveling through daily life, with the notebook as the starting point for a freer journey.

Source: https://www.travelers-company.com/travelers-company

This is memorable because the brand has one strong metaphor: travel. The product system, stores, refills, brass tools, and community all orbit that metaphor.

Do not compete with them on "notebooks for a life journey."

### Kakimori owns joy of writing and customization

Kakimori says it celebrates the joy of writing and offers notebooks, inks, pens, letter papers, order-made notebooks, and order-made inks. Its story is deeply tied to Kuramae, craftsmanship, local makers, and the pleasure of expression.

Source: https://kakimori.com/en/pages/our-story

Do not compete with Kakimori on "the joy of writing" or "custom writing tools." That is already a strong territory.

### Hobonichi owns daily life as planner culture

Hobonichi Techo is not only a planner. It is an annual ritual, a format system, a community, a cover ecosystem, and a daily habit object.

Source: https://www.1101.com/store/techo/en/about/

Do not compete with Hobonichi on planner culture, yearly life management, or daily-page ritual.

### Point of View owns curated tool experience in Seoul

Point of View Seoul is described as a concept stationery store where people experience tools from a multisensory perspective, from paper and writing instruments to objects that spark creative inspiration.

Source: https://english.visitseoul.net/shopping/POINT-OF-VIEW-SEOUL/ENP01flpb

Do not compete with Point of View on being a broad concept store or curated tool destination.

### Studio Monjo's opening

Studio Monjo can own a narrower and more memorable idea:

> a French writing house in Seoul that turns small Seoul scenes into hand-bound notebooks, monthly letters, and quiet workshops.

This is not just stationery.

It is a world-building practice around writing by hand.

## 3. Recommended Core Idea

### House Idea

> Seoul, written by hand.

This should become the memorable public idea.

It is short. It is specific. It explains why the brand is in Seoul. It connects notebooks and Courrier. It makes the founder's foreignness relevant without making the brand only about the founder. It gives Robey a world to observe.

### Emotional Expansion

> Studio Monjo makes small scenes from Seoul into objects you can write in, send, and keep.

### Practical Expansion

> Hand-bound notebooks, monthly letters, and small workshops from a French writing house in Seoul.

### French Version

> Seoul, a la main.

or:

> Des scenes de Seoul, a ecrire, envoyer et garder.

Use accents in public French copy. The ASCII version here is only for repo consistency.

### Korean Version

Use practical Korean in sales surfaces:

> 서울에서 손으로 만드는 만년필 노트와 편지

Use more poetic Korean in brand/editorial surfaces:

> 서울의 작은 장면을 손으로 쓰고, 묶고, 보냅니다.

Do not force a literal Korean slogan if it sounds unnatural. Korean can be clearer while English/French carry more atmosphere.

## 4. The New Brand Memory

After seeing Studio Monjo three times, a person should remember this:

> The French maker in Seoul who makes hand-bound notebooks and letters around small Seoul scenes. There is a little character, Robey. The objects feel quiet, tactile, and human.

That is much more memorable than:

> A nice handmade stationery brand for people who write by hand.

The brand should make people remember five things:

1. Seoul
2. hand
3. Robey
4. thread
5. letter

If every page, post, product insert, and outreach message reinforces some combination of those five signals, the brand becomes sticky.

## 5. The Ownable System

### 1. The Scene

Every emotional product should begin with a scene.

Examples:

- a robot near falling petals
- an orange balloon over Seoul
- a small library
- the June envelope leaving Seoul
- a workshop table before people arrive
- a pen moving before the laptop opens

This is the difference between generic stationery and Studio Monjo.

Generic stationery says:

> beautiful notebook

Studio Monjo says:

> this notebook began with a small Seoul scene

### 2. The Witness

Robey should not be treated as a mascot.

Robey should be the witness.

He notices Seoul. He does not explain it. He does not perform cuteness. He appears in small scenes where something almost ordinary becomes worth keeping.

Robey's job:

- make the brand recognizable
- create emotional continuity
- make Seoul feel observed, not touristic
- soften the brand without making it childish
- give people a reason to follow future editions

Robey line:

> Robey notices the small things Seoul almost lets disappear.

### 3. The Thread

The visible thread should become a signature.

Each product has a line that holds it:

- red thread for Falling Petals
- orange thread for Orange Balloon
- navy thread for Small Library
- black thread for Monjo Line

This is physical, visual, and ownable.

Use thread close-ups repeatedly. Use the thread color as the edition marker. Let the thread appear in product thumbnails, Naver banners, Instagram covers, and packaging details.

Potential phrase:

> The thread holds the scene.

Do not overuse the phrase, but let the idea guide visuals.

### 4. The Date And Place

Every Studio Monjo object should feel anchored.

Use date/place marks:

- Seoul, June 2026
- Edition 003, Small Library
- Courrier de Seoul, July 2026
- Made at the Monjo table, Seoul

This makes objects feel collected, not merely purchased.

It also makes content easier:

- "This month in Seoul"
- "This edition began on..."
- "A letter leaves Seoul in the second week"
- "At the table this Saturday"

### 5. The Bilingual Fragment

The French/Korean bridge is underused as a memorability asset.

Each Robey product should have one small bilingual fragment. Each Courrier already naturally has French; consider adding small Korean material notes or Seoul place words. Workshops can include a tiny bilingual table card.

This should not become decorative language for the sake of language. It should feel like the object passed through two worlds.

Example:

> Falling petals / les petales qui tombent / 떨어지는 꽃잎

or:

> Seoul, held in a small page.

### 6. The Maker From AI To Hand

The AI background is memorable, but it must be used sharply.

Best line:

> I work with machines that produce language. Studio Monjo is where I make room for the human hand.

Alternative:

> After years in artificial intelligence, I began making objects for the part of writing that should stay physical.

This is much more distinctive than generic founder craft language.

Use this in:

- About page
- press outreach
- one pinned founder post
- workshop introduction
- occasional long-form essay

Do not use it in every product listing. The product must still sell on its own.

## 6. Sharper Product Roles

### Robey In Seoul

Current role:

> illustrated story notebook

Sharper role:

> the small observer of Seoul

Positioning:

> Robey in Seoul is a series of hand-bound notebooks where a small character notices the Seoul scenes people might otherwise walk past.

Memory hook:

> Robey noticed it first. You write what remains.

Use cases:

- journaling
- gift
- Seoul memory
- quiet illustration
- collectible editions

### Monjo Line

Current role:

> minimal work notebook

Sharper role:

> the black-thread notebook for the thoughts before the screen

Positioning:

> Monjo Line is the quiet black-thread notebook for meetings, plans, reading notes, and the thoughts that need paper before they become digital.

Memory hook:

> Before the laptop, one page.

Use cases:

- work notes
- project thinking
- meetings
- fountain pen writing
- repeat purchase
- corporate gifting

Monjo Line needs a stronger visual symbol because it has no character. The black thread should be that symbol.

### Courrier De Seoul

Current role:

> monthly letter from Seoul

Sharper role:

> a Seoul scene sent to someone by post

Positioning:

> Courrier de Seoul is a monthly envelope for someone who wants Seoul to arrive slowly, physically, and personally.

Memory hook:

> A letter leaves Seoul.

Use cases:

- gift
- long-distance relationship
- Korea interest
- parent/friend who cannot travel
- French-speaking readers

### Workshops

Current role:

> notebook-making workshop

Sharper role:

> a seat at the Monjo table

Positioning:

> At the Monjo table, six people fold, stitch, paint, and sign one notebook by hand in Seoul.

Memory hook:

> Six seats. One table. One notebook made by hand.

Use cases:

- Seoul creative experience
- small group workshop
- private booking
- brand proof
- community

- Workshops

With:

## 8. Visual Distinctiveness

### Current Risk

The current palette is tasteful, but too much cream, quiet serif, and soft photography can become anonymous. Many premium craft brands use the same warmth.

The solution is not louder color.

The solution is stronger recurring signs.

### Recurring Signs

Use these obsessively:

1. Robey
2. thread
3. date/place marks
4. red seal
5. bilingual story card
6. founder's hands
7. envelope leaving Seoul
8. the workshop table

If an image does not contain at least one of these signals, ask whether it is generic.

### Stronger Photo Rules

Every product photo should answer one of these:

- What is the scene?
- Where is the thread?
- What can I write on?
- What leaves Seoul?
- What did the hand make?
- What will I receive?

Avoid photos that only say:

- nice paper
- nice desk
- nice light
- nice coffee
- nice mood

Mood is not enough.

### More Memorable Color Use

Use the palette like this:

- house: ink, warm paper, seal red
- Robey: warm paper plus edition thread color
- Monjo Line: white, black, black thread, one red seal
- Courrier: monthly paper color plus postal marks
- Workshop: table wood, paper, hands, tools

Build social templates around the edition thread color. A buyer should start recognizing each edition by thread before reading the caption.

## 9. Signature Content Series

### Series 1: Robey Noticed

Format:

- one small Seoul scene
- one Robey detail
- one sentence
- one notebook or sketch

Examples:

- "Robey noticed the petals before anyone swept them away."
- "Robey noticed the balloon was not lost, only going somewhere else."
- "Robey noticed a library that looked smaller from the street."

Purpose:

- build character memory
- make Seoul specific
- create low-pressure recurring content

### Series 2: Before The Laptop

Format:

- Monjo Line open on a desk
- one handwritten planning/thinking page
- one sentence about paper before screen

Examples:

- "Before the laptop, one page for the shape of the day."
- "The meeting notes that do not belong in the meeting doc yet."

Purpose:

- sell Monjo Line as practical and modern
- avoid nostalgia
- make paper useful to professionals

### Series 3: A Letter Leaves Seoul

Format:

- Courrier envelope
- monthly date
- one visual detail
- one line about who it is for

Examples:

- "A letter leaves Seoul in the second week of June."
- "For someone who wants Korea to arrive somewhere quieter than a screen."

Purpose:

- make Courrier memorable
- create monthly ritual
- strengthen gift use case

### Series 4: At The Monjo Table

Format:

- top-down workshop table
- six places or tools
- one step in the making process

Examples:

- "Six seats. One table. The first fold."
- "Today the thread goes through six hands before it holds one notebook."

Purpose:

- make workshops feel named and ownable
- lower trust barrier
- show physical experience

### Series 5: The Thread Holds

Format:

- close-up of stitch/thread
- edition color
- one practical proof detail

Examples:

- "Orange thread for the edition that looks up."
- "Black thread for the notebook that stays on the desk."

Purpose:

- make the binding a brand signature
- turn craft detail into visual memory

## 10. More Memorable Homepage Direction

Current homepage idea:

> French stationery house in Seoul. For the art of writing by hand.

Sharper homepage idea:

> Seoul, written by hand.

Subline:

> Hand-bound notebooks, monthly letters, and small workshops from a French writing house in Seoul.

Three doors:

1. Robey in Seoul  
   Small Seoul scenes, bound as notebooks.

2. Monjo Line  
   The black-thread notebook for work by hand.

3. Courrier / Workshops  
   Letters from Seoul and seats at the Monjo table.

This may be too many things for one screen, so another structure is:

1. Notebooks
2. Letters
3. Workshops

But each door gets a memorable subtitle:

- Notebooks: Robey notices Seoul. Monjo Line holds the work.
- Letters: A letter leaves Seoul each month.
- Workshops: Six seats at the Monjo table.

## 11. Stronger About Page Direction

Use this as the founder story:

> I work with machines that produce language. Studio Monjo is where I make room for the human hand.
>
> I am Jordan Monnet, a French maker in Seoul. I make hand-bound notebooks, monthly letters, and small workshops for people who still want a page, a pen, a thread, and a place for thought before it disappears.
>
> Studio Monjo began with a simple question: what is still worth making slowly, by hand, in a world where words can appear instantly?
>
> My answer is small. A notebook. A letter. A table. A scene from Seoul, held long enough for someone else to write.

This is much more memorable than a conventional maker biography.

## 12. Stronger Outreach Angle

Current outreach angle:

> I make handmade fountain-pen-friendly notebooks in Seoul.

Sharper outreach angle:

> I am a French maker in Seoul creating hand-bound notebooks and monthly letters around small Seoul scenes. The notebooks are fountain-pen-friendly, stitched by hand, and each Robey edition carries a bilingual story card.

For Monjo Line:

> I make a black-thread notebook for people who still think on paper before the screen.

For Courrier:

> I send a monthly letter from Seoul for people who want Korea to arrive as something slower than content.

For workshops:

> I host six people at a table in Seoul to fold, stitch, paint, and sign one notebook by hand.

These are more memorable because each one has a scene.

## 13. What To Make More Specific

Make these more specific everywhere:

- Seoul
- date
- thread color
- page type
- who writes in it
- where it is made
- what Robey noticed
- what leaves Seoul
- what happens at the table

Specificity is the path to memorability.

Instead of:

> A handmade notebook for writing.

Say:

> A hand-bound A6 notebook from Seoul, stitched with navy thread, for reading notes and small thoughts after the library.

Instead of:

> A monthly letter subscription.

Say:

> One envelope leaves Seoul each month with a letter, postcard, and small memory card for someone who wants to receive Korea by post.

Instead of:

> A creative workshop.

Say:

> Six seats at a Seoul table, three hours, one A6 notebook folded, stitched, painted, and signed by hand.

## 14. What To Stop Doing

Stop:

- leading with generic "writing by hand" everywhere
- using "writing objects" as the first public label
- letting cream paper and quiet type do all the branding work
- hiding the thread
- treating Robey as only a cover illustration
- treating Monjo Line as only the minimal alternative
- making workshops sound like a category instead of a named experience
- using founder story only as "I make things by hand"

Start:

- leading with Seoul
- making Robey the witness
- making thread a product signature
- making date/place marks visible
- naming the workshop table
- using "before the laptop" for Monjo Line
- using "a letter leaves Seoul" for Courrier
- using AI-to-hand as the founder contrast
